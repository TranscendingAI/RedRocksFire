# Fire Sprinkler Systems in Multi-Family Properties.

If you manage a multi-family property in Colorado — apartment, condo, townhome, or stacked student housing — the fire sprinkler system is one of the most consequential pieces of equipment on your property, and the one most likely to drift out of compliance without anyone noticing. NFPA publishes three sprinkler standards. Multi-family buildings on the Front Range usually fall under one of two of them. The third applies to most single-family homes. Knowing which standard governs your property is the difference between a passing inspection and a year of corrective-action headaches.

This guide explains how 13, 13R, and 13D apply to multi-family properties, what an annual RRFPS sprinkler inspection actually covers in Denver Metro, Lakewood, Centennial, Boulder, Fort Collins, and the surrounding AHJs, and what your records need to look like the next time a fire marshal walks through.

## NFPA 13 vs NFPA 13R vs NFPA 13D — which one governs your building

The three standards aren't interchangeable. They cover different building types, with different design philosophies behind them, and AHJs want to know you've matched the right one to the property.

### NFPA 13 — Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems

NFPA 13 is the full commercial standard. It applies to most multi-family buildings over a certain height threshold, in mixed-use occupancies (residential units above retail, for example), and in buildings with more than a small number of dwelling units on a single floor. Multi-family properties that fall under 13 typically need:

- A dedicated fire pump or a verified water supply that meets the full hazard classification
- Standpipes sized for fire department use
- Sprinkler density and spacing rules calculated to the full commercial hazard curve
- Hydraulic calculations sealed by a NICET III designer

If your building is four stories of wood-frame construction over a podium with retail below, you're almost certainly under 13.

### NFPA 13R — Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems in Low-Rise Residential Occupancies

NFPA 13R is the most common standard for multi-family residential in Colorado. It applies to residential occupancies up to and including four stories in height. The standard is intentionally less aggressive than 13 in two key ways:

- **Four-head design rule.** A 13R system only needs to be designed to discharge from no more than four sprinklers simultaneously. This usually means smaller pipe, smaller meter, and lower upfront cost — which is the whole point.
- **No requirement for standpipes or fire pumps in many cases.** Buildings under 13R rely on the sprinkler system's connection to the city water main, without needing the fire-pump redundancy that 13 mandates.

Multi-family properties that fall under 13R: walk-up apartments, garden-style apartment communities, three- and four-story residential buildings on a single municipal water supply.

### NFPA 13D — Standard for the Installation of Sprinkler Systems in One- and Two-Family Dwellings and Manufactured Homes

NFPA 13D does not apply to multi-family properties. It applies to single-family homes, duplexes, and manufactured homes. If you've seen 13D cited in the context of "all residential fire sprinklers," that's the source — and it's the wrong standard for any property you're managing.

### How to know which one you have

If you're not sure which standard governed the original install, there are two ways to find out:

1. **The original plans and the AHJ stamp on them.** Every multi-family sprinkler plan in Colorado is reviewed and stamped by the AHJ before install. That stamp cites the standard the system was designed under. The original stamped set should be in your project file.
2. **Ask a NICET III-certified contractor.** A qualified inspection company will look at the riser, the supply, the design calc, and tell you within an hour. RRFPS can do this as part of a routine inspection.

If the wrong standard was cited on the original install, that's not necessarily a demolition sentence — it can sometimes be brought into compliance with retrofits — but you want to know about it before a fire marshal tells you.

## What the annual inspection actually covers

Annual sprinkler inspection in Colorado typically references NFPA 25 — the Standard for the Inspection, Testing, and Maintenance of Water-Based Fire Protection Systems. NFPA 25 is the rule book AHJs use to evaluate whether your system is in working order, regardless of which installation standard (13, 13R, or 13D) your property was built under.

For a 13R multi-family property, the typical annual inspection touches:

- **Main drain test.** Opens the main drain valve and reads the static and residual pressure. Drop in static pressure between tests tells us about supply restrictions — closed valves partially down the line, internal pipe scaling, or — in extreme cases — partially closed curb-side valves.
- **Wet-pipe gauge checks.** Every gauge on the system has a calibration window. Expired gauges get replaced, and the readings get logged.
- **Valve supervision.** Every control valve needs to be in the open position and locked or electronically supervised. We physically visit and tag every valve as part of the inspection.
- **Sprinkler head visual inspection.** We're looking for corrosion, paint, dust, or physical damage. Painted heads and corroded heads in unconditioned attic spaces are the most common deficiencies we find.
- **Hazard change review.** If a tenant has remodeled a unit and changed the hazard (added a commercial kitchen, moved storage, blocked a sprinkler path), that needs to be flagged.
- **Dry-pipe, preaction, or antifreeze system specifics.** Properties with unheated attic or exterior-canopy coverage often have dry or antifreeze systems. Those have additional trip tests, low-point drain checks, and antifreeze concentration tests.

The deliverable is a written report — with photos where there's a deficiency — that your AHJ, your lender, and your insurance underwriter can all accept.

## How Colorado AHJs handle deficiencies

Multi-family sprinkler deficiencies in Colorado typically fall into three buckets, and which bucket a deficiency lands in drives the timeline for getting it corrected.

1. **Critical — addressed within days.** Blocked drains, closed valves, severely corroded or painted heads, water supply pressure below the system curve. AHJs will sometimes red-tag a system on the spot if a critical deficiency creates immediate life-safety exposure. We've seen AHJs issue a 30-day correction order for less severe cases.

2. **Non-critical — addressed within 90 days.** Most common deficiencies: minor corrosion on branch lines in unconditioned spaces, sprinkler heads that have been painted over in tenant remodels, gauges approaching calibration window expiry, missing signage at control valves.

3. **Record-keeping only — noted in the report, no AHJ follow-up expected.** Tags and labels that have faded but are still readable. Floor signs that have been moved during a remodel. Cosmetic concerns.

The RRFPS report classifies every finding into one of those three buckets, with photos and a recommended action. When your AHJ asks for the file, you hand them the report and the receipt showing the correction-in-progress — that's usually sufficient to demonstrate good-faith compliance.

## What changes between multi-family and single-family standards

If you've read our fire alarm, fire extinguisher, or general inspection coverage, you'll see us lean on NFPA 72, NFPA 10, and similar umbrella standards the same way. For sprinklers specifically:

- **Single-family properties** (most townhomes, single-family rentals, and duplexes in Colorado) are governed by NFPA 13D. The annual test cadence under NFPA 25 is lighter, and the system itself is simpler — there is rarely a fire pump and rarely a backflow preventer that needs annual certification.
- **Multi-family properties** under NFPA 13R have a heavier annual test cadence — main drain tests, gauge calibrations, valve supervision, and visual on every head in every unit above a ceiling or in an attic.
- **Multi-family properties** under NFPA 13 are commercial-class systems. They usually include a fire pump that gets its own annual test, and may include a standpipe that gets hydrostatic testing on a 5-year cycle.

We size our inspection scope to the standard that actually applies to your property. Properties that started residential and converted to mixed-use often have a system built under one standard and an occupancy that triggers a different standard — that's the situation where a one-hour walk-through from a qualified inspector is worth more than a year of guesswork.

The intent is that the next person who needs to look at the inspection file — a lender, a successor property manager, an AHJ on turnover, an insurance adjuster after an incident — has everything they need in a single document.

## When to schedule

The most common question property managers ask is "When should I have my sprinkler inspection done?" Two answers:

1. **At least 90 days before your AHJ renewal date.** Most Colorado jurisdictions require annual inspections to land in a specific window relative to your last inspection, and the AHJ typically reviews the report before they re-issue a permit or certificate. Doing the inspection 90 days early gives you room to correct any deficiencies before the AHJ gets involved.
2. **Coordinate with your fire alarm and extinguisher inspections.** Scheduling the sprinkler, alarm, and extinguisher inspections in the same visit — what we call a coordinated walk-through — saves you a day of tenant disruption, a round of access coordination, and a stack of separate reports to reconcile.
